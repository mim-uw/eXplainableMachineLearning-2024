Report¶

Task 1¶

Demographic parity:¶

$P(\hat{Y}=1|A=Blue) = \frac{65}{100} = \frac{13}{20}$

$P(\hat{Y}=1|A=Red) = \frac{1}{2}$

A random person from the Blue group has a probability of 65% to qualify for the course and a random person from the Red group only 50%. As you can see, $\frac{P(\hat{Y}=1|A=Blue)}{P(\hat{Y}=1|A=Red)}=130\%$, so this decision rule doesn't satisfy "four-fifth rule".

Equal opportunity:¶

$P(\hat{Y}=1|A=Blue, Y=1) = \frac{60}{80} = \frac{3}{4}$

$P(\hat{Y}=1|A=Red, Y=1) = \frac{1}{2}$

$\frac{P(\hat{Y}=1|A=Blue,Y=1)}{P(\hat{Y}=1|A=Red,Y=1)}=150\%$

If we only consider people who will use the XAIa in the future then the above inequality becomes even more pronounced. Of the Blue people who will use XAIa, 75% will be qualified for the course. For Red people, it will be only 50%. This clearly shows that the described decision rule also doesn't satisfy the equal opportunity.

Predictive rate parity:¶

$P(Y=1|A=Blue,\hat{Y}=1) = \frac{60}{65} = \frac{12}{13}$

$P(Y=1|A=Red, \hat{Y}=1) = \frac{1}{2}$

$P(Y=1|A=Blue,\hat{Y}=0) = \frac{20}{35} = \frac{4}{7}$

$P(Y=1|A=Red, \hat{Y}=0) = \frac{1}{2}$

We can see that virtually all of the Blue qualifiers on the course will use XAIa, but this property does not work for the Red qualifiers. The second condition, it is almost fulfilled, because $P(Y=1|A=Blue,\hat{Y}=0)\approx P(Y=1|A=Red, \hat{Y}=0)$, but this is not enough to say that the predictive rate parity property to occur.

Better solution¶

A better decision rule would be to draw people from group Red with a probability of 65%, as this would satisfy the principle of demographic parity. Of course, it would be best to come up with a way of selecting people from group Red that would actually state whether a person would need an XAI knowledge in the future.

Task 2¶

I decided to select a set of adult income and determine whether XGBoost trained on it would give fair predictions, independent of age and gender. I divided the set into a training set and a test set in a ratio of 3:1. I set the following parameters in the model: number of estimators equal to 50 and maximum depth of 2. I chose AUC as the metric for evaluation. For the model thus trained, I calculated the fairness coefficients using the package "dalex". I considered men over 30 years of age as the privileged group and compared them with the other three groups. I present the results in the graph below.

As can be seen, the indicators clearly differ for each group. The greatest differences can be seen between older and younger people. If we look at the equal opportunity ratio, we see that none of the results are within the norm of (0.8, 1.25).

I also decided to see how the coefficients would change after training the model with other parameters. I increased the number of estimators to 200 and the maximum depth to 4. I also changed the metric for evaluation to logloss. It turned out that such a model is not only better in terms of accuracy and AUC, but also gives better fairness coefficients. I present the results below.

My final task was to compare the first model with a model that reduces bias. To do this, I trained XGBoost once more (with the initial parameters), but removed the gender and age information from the data. I recalculated all the coefficients and placed them on the comparison chart.

The biggest difference between the models can be seen for the equal opportunity ratio, which performed much better with the new model and is almost within the designated standard. Unfortunately, the other ratios performed very similarly and are not satisfactory results. This shows that the model is able to favour certain groups even when it does not get information about the individuals' group membership. This is most likely due to the specificity of the data.

I compared the results for the individual models and did not find a strong correlation between fairness coefficients and results. The model with increased parameters proved to be the best. The model without gender and age information did the worst (because it got fewer predictors), but in terms of accuracy and AUC these differences are very small.
