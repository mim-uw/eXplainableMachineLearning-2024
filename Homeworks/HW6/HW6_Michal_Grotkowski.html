The results above showcase two classes of models: tree-based and linear model based. Tree-based models can take into account relationships between variables while the logistic regression model cannot. We can see that both tree-based models: XGBoost and Random Forest show the same three variables with highest PVI values. Logistic Regression overlaps in 2/3 of these variables, giving a higher importance to educational-num than age, compared to tree-based model. This may be a result of not capturing a relationship between one of those top variables or a smaller coefficient assigned to age variable resulting in a smaller loss difference.
There is another case with XGBoost with a smaller learning rate that performs the worst out of the 4 models. It also does not acknowledge the age in the top 3 features. The smaller learning rate and thus slower convergence may have not allowed the model to create a desired split to capture the variable's importance.

XGBoost PVI comparision with other importance estimation methods

The top 5 most important features mostly overlap between PVI and averaged Shapley values and gain-based importance estimations. What is interesting the gain results do not include the age variable, which is featured in a high spot in other importance metrics. This may be due to the particular nature of gain computation, where a split is made in order to estimate the accuracy gain. This approach may value binary features more highly as there are fewer splits to make, compared to age variable which has more values. This problem is not seen in Shapley estimates as this method is based on sampling.
